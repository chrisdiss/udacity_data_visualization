Trip Duration

How Long Do People Ride?

Trip durations follow a right-skewed distribution — most rides are short urban hops under 15 minutes, with a long tail of extended journeys pulling the mean above the median.

Trip Duration Distribution
Number of trips by duration bucket
Key Finding: Most trips cluster between 3–12 minutes. The right skew is driven by casual Customer riders taking extended leisure journeys, pulling the mean 40% above the median.
User Segments

Subscribers vs Customers

Two fundamentally different user groups define the network. Subscribers dominate in volume. Customers dominate in ride length — taking nearly twice as long per trip, distributed evenly across the entire system.

Temporal Patterns

When Does the City Ride?

Usage patterns reveal the service's rhythm: twin commuter spikes at 8 AM and 5 PM on weekdays, with weekend demand dropping by over 60% — the unmistakable signature of a commuter-dominated service.

Trips by Hour of Day
Average trip count per hour · February 2019
Rush Hour Peaks: Demand spikes sharply at 8:00 AM and again at 5:00–6:00 PM, perfectly mirroring San Francisco office commute hours. Off-peak hours see 10–15× fewer trips.
Trips by Day of Week
Total trips per weekday · February 2019
Weekday Dominance: Tuesday–Thursday are peak days. Friday drops ~15% from midweek. Saturday and Sunday see 60–77% fewer trips — confirming commuter-driven demand over recreational use.

Cluster Analysis
What the three clusters reveal
🏙️ Downtown Hub (Cluster 1)
Stations 58, 67, and 81 are the three busiest in the entire network. They serve as primary commuter entry points — Station 58 alone sees 3,904 trips in one month.
🏘️ Mission District (Cluster 2)
Residential neighborhood stations show consistent morning outbound and evening inbound flows — a pure commuter signature with predictable daily rhythms.
🌊 Embarcadero (Cluster 3)
Waterfront stations attract a mix of commuters and casual riders, with more balanced usage throughout the day compared to pure rush-hour spikes in Cluster 1.

Key Insights

5 Findings That Matter

Distilled from 183,412 trips — the most actionable patterns the data reveals about the Ford GoBike network.

2×
Customers ride twice as long as Subscribers
The starkest behavioral divide in the data. Customers average ~19.8 min per trip; subscribers ~10.1 min. This isn't location-specific — customers are distributed across the entire system.
3
Three station clusters drive the network
Stations organize into 3 tightly interconnected clusters. Within-cluster connectivity is extremely high; inter-cluster travel is rarer — meaning the network has clear functional zones.
8am
Morning rush hour is the critical demand window
8 AM sees the single highest demand of any hour. Bike availability at Cluster 1 stations during this window is the most important operational factor for service quality.
89%
Subscribers dominate, but Customers are underserved
While subscribers make up 89% of trips, the casual segment has distinct needs. A one-size-fits-all approach systematically underserves this 11% — likely including tourists and new residents.
3,904
Station 58 is a critical single point of failure
Station 58 handles 3,904 trips/month — more than 2× the average. A shortage of bikes here during rush hour could cascade into significant, measurable service degradation.
74%
The gender gap represents a growth opportunity
Women account for only 23% of trips despite representing ~50% of the Bay Area population. Addressing infrastructure and safety barriers could nearly double the addressable user base.
Recommendations

Turning Data into Action

The analysis reveals four concrete operational improvements that can immediately improve service quality and reduce friction for both user segments.

01
🚲
Dynamic Bike Rebalancing
Pre-position bikes at Cluster 1 stations (especially 58, 67, 81) before the 8 AM and 5 PM rush. Demand is predictable and consistent — rebalancing should be proactive, not reactive.
02
🔗
Manage Clusters as Systems
Stop treating stations in isolation. The three clusters are interconnected micro-networks — inventory decisions at one node affect the others. Optimize at the cluster level, not the individual station level.
03
👤
Differentiate by User Segment
Subscribers and Customers have fundamentally different needs. Subscribers need speed and reliability. Customers benefit from route suggestions, tourist waypoints, and flexible pricing. Build separate UX flows.
04
♀️
Close the Gender Gap
With women at only 23% of riders vs. ~50% of the population, this is a significant untapped market. Investigate infrastructure safety gaps and launch targeted programs to reduce barriers to entry.
